September 2025 · Personal Essay

The Torch My Grandfather Never Carried

Originally published in Indonesian in the Kominfo-Bitread anthology Aku Bangga Asian Games di Negeriku (Writingthon Asian Games Pelajar 2018), within the title of "Asian Games di Indonesia: Harus Bangga atau Kecewa?" Translated and adapted into English by the author for this portfolio.

By Mahdi Singaparado

"You may break the old Pattimuras, bind us beneath the equator's heat," my grandfather sang, his voice coarse, almost breaking, as though the poem had to be dragged from deep inside his lungs. "But the young Pattimuras will rise again, like ships charging through storm."

The syair belonged to a revolutionary generation, yet it reached me through the thin wire of a telephone, from Bima to Jakarta, from the old to the young. I wanted to listen, but impatience betrayed me.

"Grandfather, how did you feel when Indonesia hosted the Asian Games in 1962?"

A pause, then a sigh, long enough that I thought the line had dropped.

"Disappointed," he said.

Not proud. Not triumphant. Disappointed.

I grew up with the opposite story. In my textbooks, 1962 was radiant: Sukarno erecting Gelora Bung Karno Stadium, the young republic dressing itself for continental glory, Indonesia asserting itself against imperial powers. The Games were meant as proof that Asia's newest nation was no longer a colonial subject but a leader. But my grandfather's word cut through that official pageantry like a shadow across a floodlit track.

The Bicycle and The City

In 1961, he was not yet my grandfather, only Yasin, eighteen years old, carrying his bicycle across islands by train, five days from Surabaya to the capital. He had one intention: to ride for his country.

Jakarta was a fever then—roads torn open for stadiums, workers hauling limestone to meet impossible deadlines. Sukarno, ever the architect-president, called it nation-building, but for many it was literally so: stadiums and overpasses rising overnight, funded by Soviet rubles, engineered with the urgency of geopolitics. The Games were never only about sport; they were about prestige in the Cold War, about Bandung's legacy of Afro-Asian solidarity, about proving that Indonesia could speak to the world in its own voice.

But for Yasin, it was only about the bicycle.

He circled Senayan every morning, pedaling past half-complete structures that smelled of wet cement. He queued outside offices, slipped his name into informal lists, sought rumors of open trials. He imagined himself inside the velodrome, his legs not simply turning pedals but turning history itself.

Each time, the answer was silence. The team was already chosen, though no one said it aloud. Athletics, like politics, was an economy of access, of names known to names. So he sold newspapers instead, pedaling before dawn, delivering the headlines of his own exclusion. Each morning he flung bundles into gated yards, their front pages proclaiming national readiness, Asian solidarity, Sukarno's speeches, the promise of glory. The irony was suffocating: the very boy who believed in that promise became the invisible courier of its story.

The Collision

On 17 August 1962—Independence Day—the city erupted with flags, bands, and parades. Jakarta wore its nationalism like a new suit, eager for the Games that would open days later.

Yasin pedaled across Medan Merdeka Barat with his last stack of papers. The air was filled with trumpets, the smell of smoke from fireworks, the nervous joy of a young nation rehearsing its grandeur. From the left, a black car came. The impact was sudden, merciless.

The bicycle shattered. His body folded. Bone broke. He was carried not to the stadium but to the hospital.

There, in a ward that smelled of iodine and metal, he listened to the Games open. Radio Republik Indonesia carried the roar of the stadium, Sukarno's speech, the ignition of the torch. But it reached him only as static, a ghost of applause that seeped through walls.

Indonesia surged forward, radiant on the continental stage. Yasin lay still, erased from it.

He never rode again.

After the Dream

What do you do when the dream collapses under the weight of your own bones? My grandfather chose to reassemble himself into someone else: a teacher.

"To be an athlete," he once told me, "is to defend the nation through victory. To be a teacher is to defend it through knowledge. Both are necessary."

I have often wondered whether this was conviction or consolation. Perhaps both. Trauma rearranges not only the body but also the narrative one tells about life. For him, the chalkboard became a substitute for the velodrome; classrooms replaced stadiums. His authority was no longer timed in seconds but measured in lessons imparted, seeds planted in the soil of younger minds.

Inheritance of Disappointment

When he told me his story, I felt it enter me like an inheritance—not only the narrative but the disappointment itself. It is strange, this intergenerational transmission of grief: how a bicycle accident in 1962 could echo in the chest of a grandson born decades later.

And yet, even as I absorbed it, I resisted. His world was one of patience, process, obedience to structure. Mine was one of speed, improvisation, irreverence. He accepted bitterness as if it were part of the duty of adulthood; I recoil from bitterness, convinced it corrodes more than it teaches.

Where he endured the silence of locked doors, I demand explanation. Where he lived with unfulfilled longing, I choose to transmute longing into expression.

Writing as Spectatorship

I will never pedal in Senayan. I will never carry the torch. But I can write, and perhaps writing is its own form of spectatorship, a cheer that vibrates not in stadiums but in sentences.

In this way, I stand both with him and against him: inheriting the weight of his disappointment but refusing to let it calcify. The Asian Games for me is not a site of betrayal but of possibility. My words become the applause he could not hear, the recognition he was denied.

Torchlines

The Asian Games were always about more than sport. For Sukarno, they were a statement of sovereignty, a way of writing Indonesia into global modernity. For my grandfather, they were a personal catastrophe, a dream undone by a black car and a broken leg. For me, they are both: proof of national triumph and evidence of its exclusions.

When my grandfather sang of the young Pattimuras rising again, I used to think he was speaking about athletes, revolutionaries, men of physical endurance. Now I think he was also speaking of us—the inheritors of disappointment, the ones who transmute failure into other forms of strength.

If he carried no torch, then perhaps I must. Not in a stadium, but on the page.
