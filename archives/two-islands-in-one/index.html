Two Islands in One: Of Legends, Local Eats, and the Stories They Keep

Exploring hidden histories, local flavors, and truths beyond Lombok's tourist gaze

By Mahdi Singaparado

Dual Histories: Between Guidebooks and Whispers

Every island has two histories: the one written for visitors, and the one whispered to you over coffee by someone who inherits the stories like a heirloom. It's like two sides of a coin. The first half is glossy, its edges smoothed for easy consumption, customized for guidebooks and tourist brochures that leave no reason for anyone not to be mesmerized. The other half, though less fashionable, is resistant, fragile, and most of the time—uncomfortable. It hides in conversations, in recipes, and in quiet pauses when locals decide whether to let you in.

A clear example of that notion is the story of Mandalika. Tourists see a noble sacrifice; locals whisper curses. Between these two histories lies her scandal—a truth I hadn't yet grasped when I first arrived. I didn't come to Lombok seeking enlightenment. I came with messy notes, unfinished drafts, and a craving to feel alive again after a long season of writer's block.

If anything, I came like you come to a friend's house when you're too tired to explain yourself: half-expecting warmth, half-ready to collapse on the couch. And that's exactly how Lombok met me—not with spa resorts and curated sunsets, but with rain, a broken phone, and the kind of chaos you don't post on Instagram.

Rain, Blurriness & Doorway to Truth

It was raining hard when I landed last December. The kind of rain that body-slams you the second you step out of the terminal. Ten minutes later, in the airport toilet, my phone slipped straight out of my jacket's chest pocket and dove into the closet like it had unfinished business there. The water blurred the camera permanently. Thanks to my ADHD, I had completely forgotten the phone was even there when I sat down.

Hadid, my best friend who had promised to pick me up, took one look at my sulking face and burst out laughing before dragging me straight to a repair shop. He has this way of being both amused and sympathetic at the same time.

"Couldn't say nothing less," he said, glancing at the blurry screen where the world swam like an old VHS tape.

"I know, classic me," I lifted up phone, gave it to the repairman. "It's like it had just come back from a bad ayahuasca trip."

Then, almost as if the moment had been waiting for him, he added: "You know, Mandalika threw herself into the sea too. Sometimes when things blur, it's not an accident. It's a choice."

I frowned out of bizarre dazedness. "You mean I'm a gag finna be letting my phone drowned on a damp? Or does that mean Mandalika actually had a choice when people assume she jumped out of desperation?"

Hadid didn't answer at first. There was silence, and he started to look serious, knocking the wooden table as he started to tell that story. And just like that, my broken camera lens became a doorway. One minute I was worried about my phone, the next I was listening to him summon the island's most haunting legend—one that no guidebook could have prepared me for.

Mandalika Revealed: Myths Unfolded in Coffee and Rain

The official story is easy to admire. Princess Mandalika was so beautiful that men from across kingdoms came to fight for her hand. Rather than let them kill each other, she climbed to a cliff above the sea and leapt into the waves, her body dissolving into Nyale—colorful sea worms that appear once a year. Today, the Bau Nyale Festival gathers thousands who collect the worms, cook them into dishes, and celebrate her eternal sacrifice.

It's the kind of story that fits neatly into tourist brochures: noble, tragic, clean. But my bestie tilted the tale on its head. "That's not the whole truth," he said, sipping coffee as if what he was about to say was casual, not explosive. He told me that every Nyale season, people curse Mandalika. They see her not as a heroine, but as a metaphor for promiscuity. Splitting herself into worms for everyone was, in their telling, proof that she "let herself" be taken by all men.

"People call her cunt," he said flatly.

I sat there, the blur of my phone screen almost mocking me. Was it really her fault? Was she guilty for being beautiful, for inspiring hunger in men? Or were they guilty, unable to master their desires, their violence? The version of Mandalika I met in that kitchen was not a legend on a pedestal but a woman trapped in other people's greed.

The River, The Jam, and Lombok's Flavors

If Mandalika's story was the island's mythic blur, then Jonggat was the sharpness that followed. Hadid somehow became my custodian of local knowledge, folding me into Lombok's rhythms, as I felt myself becoming a kin to him. There's a difference between being a guest and being part of a household: a guest gets served. Kin is handed a basket and told to come along because the river spinach won't pick itself.

We walked past ricefields laced with waterways, the kind of ancient irrigation system that looks casual until you realize it has sustained generations. I was mesmerized by how the water flowed—crystal clear and fresh.

"Call me whatever you want," I said, still amazed. "But I'm obsessed with this view. You'll never see anything like it in Jakarta."

Hadid shrugged, a faint smile playing on his lips. "Jakarta doesn't care about green. Only gray."

Around us, coconut trees lined the edges like silent guardians; the sky remaining calmed by the damp scent carried by the earlier rain; and the earth glowed green—lush in the way only the tropics know how to be. Hadid led me down a narrow path to the river behind his house. The water rushed gently over smooth stones, cold and clear. We waded in together, plucking kangkung from the current, its long stems slippery and soft in my hands.

Back at the house, we harvested jambolan fruits from the tree next to his house. It became a sticky purple jam that we smeared onto pancakes, though our first batch almost failed. Hadid accidentally added too much cornstarch, and it became more like agar-agar than jam. We panicked instantly, then laughing ourselves into calm.

The plecing kangkung is prepared in the authentic way. "No onion," Hadid insists. "I've always been annoyed by how many people sells the altered version of it."

I teased, "You mean your culinary kingdom must be preserved at all costs?"

"Of course!" he said, grinding fresh chili, garlic, Lombok's terasi, and tomato in a cobek. "Because every bite is a small rebellion." I then folded in grated coconut and a fresh lime juice, then mixed it well with the boiled water spinach.

My mouth is drooling. The lunch is served and I devoured it. In a perfect rhythm, my tongue burned, cooled, acidified, and burned again. The whole flavor was creamy and bright by the plecing-kangkung, smoky with quite a strong hints of tartness and savory of grilled tilapia, the burnt spices from sate lilit's seasoning, and the taste of ayam taliwang mixed with three different sambals that leaves a lasting aftertaste with a savory texture that smells just like eating sunlight tempered by sea wind.

I arrived at the conclusion that Lombok's food was more like an island translated into flavor than a curated pieces of meals without soul. It's unapologetically alive, and bold, and layered. Sorry, Manado and Minang foods, Lombok catches my tongue better.

As I ate, Mandalika returned to me. Her story felt uncomfortably parallel to the meal before me—something consumed, something pulled apart and made to serve others. Yet, in the intimacy of that kitchen, she felt closer, less like a myth and more like a misunderstood woman.

Between Myth and Life: Lesson from Ancestors of Pujut Nobles

Maybe Lombok's truths are like its food. They don't soften themselves to please outsiders. They sting, surprise, demand your attention. They're messy, imperfect, unforgettable. And once you taste them, you can't return to the bland version served elsewhere.

I thought of guidebooks, their neat bullet points about temples and beaches, and realized how pale they were compared to the Lombok Hadid had handed me. History here was not a polished monument—it was a living, breathing mess of contradictions, flavors, and wounds.

When I confessed to Hadid about leaving Jakarta due to my writer's block—the exhaustion, the weight, the choice to leave—he reminded me of the history of Pujut nobles who fled Balinese pursuit, escaping Kuta on the southern coast and traveling north until they settled in Jonggat.

"It's in Bonjeruk, their settlement. Remember the housing complex we passed when I drove you to the repair shop? The one in the style of old Sasak with red brick walls?" he asked. I then tried revisiting my memories of the other day, and I nodded. "Their descendants still live there," Hadid continued. "But they rarely open to outsiders."

I was still on the silence when Hadid gave me that punchy piece of advice.

"You see, sometimes running away isn't about cowardice. It's an act of love for yourself. They left treasure, homes, even titles, because some things are worth more than possession. It is devotion, bloodline, love."

I held his words like a mirror. Leaving was not defeat, and it was not an escapism. If anything, it was an act of care to honor the parts of myself that needed room to breathe. In that quiet, ancestral echo, I understood how myth, history, and personal choice could converge into wisdom.

The Marese Hill Sunset: A Cinematic Closure of the Journey

On our final evening, Hadid took me to Marese Hill, the road cutting past small villages where the sea peeked between palm groves. At the top, the light had softened, spilling across the slopes, highlighting the blades of grass and the tide below. The air smelled faintly of salt and damp earth from the day's rain.

We unpacked the pancakes we had carried, smeared with the purple "semi-jam" we almost ruined. Though it's too stiff to spread properly, it still tasted sweet enough to redeem itself. Monkeys wandered the hilltop, quiet but alert, their silhouettes moving against the fading light. Two eagles appeared above, gliding in slow arcs across the horizon. I laughed at the scene—how could such a perfect scene be real?

From Hadid's small speaker, Lana Del Rey's voice drifted, languid and aching, dissolving into the air like incense.

"The scandal of Mandalika, the nobles of Pujut, Lale Gande Asbe Sabe Gande Sari, Lalu Hang Suluh Dipa Benlya, Lale Zahzang Sani Puri," Hadid said suddenly, almost like a chant.

I turned to him, frowning. "What?"

"They're real people," he shrugged. "Friends, or friends of friends. Some are descendants of those Pujut nobles I told you about."

"And what's that supposed to mean?" I asked.

"You're stuck on your novel, right?" he said. "Maybe the answer's here. These names, these stories—they could be the gate to your worldbuilding. You just need to listen to them."

Something clicked. I gasped, wide-eyed, as if the names themselves cracked open a door I had been too tired to push.

That sunset became the most important moment of my 2024. Not because it was flawless, but because of its fractures: the broken lens, the failed jam, the whispered scandals, the scattered confessions, the names carrying centuries of memory. Together, they gave me clarity—an answer I had been chasing in silence.

And so I sat on that hill, eating imperfect pancakes, watching eagles slice the sky, while Lana sang us into the dark. It was not a postcard—it was a vision, and it was mine.

Every island has two histories. On Lombok—the one Hadid's ancestors inherited to him, and he inherited to me—I was lucky enough to hear both.
